# Competitive Programming

Competitive programming (CP) is simply a world of possibilities. I've come to this conclusion after finishing the Advanced Topics in Programming Languages (TALP) course, but I won't stop here. My goal is to stay active solving problems, because it's become a hobby for me — almost a new addiction. I say that because over the past few months I went through a change and realized I could turn this into a new habit, and in doing so, leave a harmful one behind. So far, I can say I've managed to do that. Being forced to put my brain to work, search for alternatives, recall concepts, and apply knowledge from computer science courses and mathematical properties (among many other things) is genuinely challenging.

More than a year ago (I'm writing this on July 8, 2026, at 9:50 PM), I watched my friend [Vinícius Castro](https://github.com/moreiracvinicius) dive headfirst into the world of competitive programming, and I barely understood any of it. A few times I saw Vinícius meet up with [Gabriel dos Santos](https://github.com/gabrielduzz) and [Artur Rodrigues](https://github.com/ArturRodriguesM), both in our apartment and at UESB. They have a team, Deadlock. My special thanks to the three of them in advance. So, thanks to the initiative of these friends, with the support of Professor Marlos Marques, in the 2026.1 semester we had the Advanced Topics in Programming Languages course — an introduction to competitive programming. I was thrilled when I heard the news.

The course delivered far more than I expected. It was, by far, the best course I've taken at university, and I have to say so. It was very well organized, fun, challenging, and had a quality of teaching I hadn't seen before at UESB. [Artur](https://github.com/ArturRodriguesM), [Gabriel](https://github.com/gabrielduzz), and [Vinícius](https://github.com/moreiracvinicius) proved to be excellent teaching assistants — professors, really. In my opinion, it was clear they not only mastered the content but also excelled at explaining every topic. I'm also grateful to them for their availability — they always cleared up my doubts without leaving anything unanswered — and for doing it all for free. All the materials used throughout the course are available in the [TALP 2026.1 repository](https://github.com/gabrielduzz/TALP-2026.1/tree/main).

As for me, I dedicated myself heavily to TALP this semester. I definitely went well beyond the course's 60 hours. I also succeeded by staying very close to the problems, solving them almost daily. Living with Vinícius Castro helped a lot too, haha. At first, I struggled a bit — which is normal — readjusting to C++, the language most widely used in the CP world. After that, I started solving many Codeforces problems by following the TLE Eliminators playlists. This semester, I managed to get through the 1000 and 1100 rating playlists.

The classes were also very conducive to developing competitive thinking, with every assessment structured as a contest. I even placed first in one of them — who would've guessed. Today (July 8th), it wrapped up for me with the practical team exam (contest). We have a team: **Os Menino de Vó**, made up of [Augusto César](https://github.com/augusto-csa), [Luan Alves](https://github.com/luanlelis12), and me (Gustavo Cruz). We'll be competing in the first phase of the SBC Programming Marathon this coming August, in Guanambi — a city I love so much.

Finally, finishing this course with a 100% grade is something I've never achieved before at UESB. I'm truly proud of it. Thank God I earned this achievement — and thanks also to the quality and organization of the course. As I said, I'll keep trying to solve more problems; I don't want to stop. I'll be adding some of them here, to this repository I've been building since the start of the course — I've included every problem I solved during this period, and there will be many more.
